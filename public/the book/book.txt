The Trading Tribe
Welcome


Welcome to The Trading Tribe. We meet periodically in small groups to work on ourselves, so we may better serve others.


This book shows what the Tribe does and how it works. This website makes it easy to join or start a Tribe in your area. 


Like many organisms, the Tribe has many parts that fit together and depend on each other. To tell this story, I start out with this song about how we all need some work and a Tribe. We all need to share deep feelings, to see things grow and we need love, real love—the kind that expresses our deep mental, emotional, physical, and spiritual interconnections.


Today, many people live without meaningful work or a supportive Tribe; they judge and hide their feelings, and instead of growing their relationships and serving others, they lock themselves into depression, addictive drugs, and other forms of self-destructive drama.  


The Trading Tribe offers a way for people to learn how to heal trauma, rebuild their basic human connections, and nourish the endemic hunger for community. You might think of the Tribe as a cauldron into which Tribe members jump and swirl around with each other and experience the alchemy of forging a healing community from separate individuals.  


The main ingredients for the recipe: sharing feelings, active listening, staying in the moment of now, practicing vulnerability, releasing judgment, viewing the overall system, celebrating our deep connections, and serving others. This book gets into the details of how to mix everything together. Still, and like a recipe, it cannot give you the actual flavor. For that, you have to jump in and swirl around with the rest of us.


As you continue reading along, you may occasionally encounter an unfamiliar term or concept. I recommend you pause reading and consult the Chief (my on-line clone) or send me feedback; see the links below the book. In this way, you can maintain clarity all the way through the book, and I can get some feedback from the Chief about how to make the book easier to read. Like a Tribe Member, this book works on itself and gets stronger so as to better serve others.


Tribe members generally use SVOP syntax. SVOP stands for Subject, Verb, Object, Present-Tense. I use SVOP syntax in this book. Every sentence has a subject, a descriptive verb in the present tense, and an object. SVOP supports thinking and operating in the ever-evolving moment of now. 


Some people compare the Trading Tribe to therapy or a service organization or a club or even a cult. Next, we take a look at some of these comparisons.

Kind of Like


Three Blind Men and an Elephant


Three blind men happen upon an elephant. The first bumps into the elephant’s leg and thinks, “Kind of like a tree.” The second encounters the elephant’s trunk and thinks “Kind of like a snake.” The third feels the elephant’s tail and thinks, “Kind of like a rope.” They all meet and compare notes and agree to call it a snake-rope tree.


Kind of Like Group Therapy


Tribe resembles group therapy as people meet and help each other evolve. However, it does not follow the classic therapist-patient model. Tribe members provide a Healing Field of Acknowledgement for each other that validates their feelings and helps them dissolve their judgments, get past their defenses, and reframe their feelings from adversaries to allies. 








Healing Field of Acknowledgment


The relentless validation 
of each other’s feelings
provides a crucible 
in which to dissolve judgment.


	



Tribe members come to view their interactions with others as cooperative systems and practice ways to make these systems work better. In relationship with others, members reprogram their deep and automatic response patterns. They come to notice love naturally passing through everyone, including themselves, and that loving heals and carries little risk; not loving carries the risk. Some Tribe members also have therapists and find Tribe work nicely complements and accelerates their therapy.


Kind of Like a Church


Tribe resembles a church as it acknowledges spiritual connection, engages prayer, fosters community, and promotes serving others. However, it does not personify any one specific brand of higher power, offer redemption from sin in exchange for accepting a savior, promise deliverance, proselytize, or pass a collection plate. 


Tribe provides community, opportunities for deep sharing with others and mutual accountability—areas that complement and supplement most spiritual practices. Tribe members meet in small intimate support groups of about eight persons and sit in a circle, facing each other, whereas church members normally meet in groups of hundreds and sit in rows facing the religious leader.


Tribe members acknowledge the existence of a higher power and also acknowledge that different people may have different religions, faiths, deities, and different ways to pray. The Trading Tribe acknowledges and accepts all major religions, sciences, and philosophies. Tribe members pray, in their own ways, mostly for guidance, rarely for results. Prayer also helps Tribe members maintain attitudes of humility and gratitude.




  





















Mycelia Network
Source: Gemini Art


Mycelia form a network of thread-like fungal strands in soil enabling trees to communicate and send nutrients and water to each other through the substrate. Some people claim ingesting psilocybin, a psychedelic compound from “magic” mushrooms, induces a state of spiritual connection.   


Kind of Like a Motivational Series


Tribe resembles a motivational series as it supports its members in setting goals and working toward realizing them, in finding a balance between seeking success and avoiding failure, in finding satisfaction inside rather than seeking it externally, and in staying constant to purpose despite short-term setbacks.


The Tribe supports its members in discovering their callings, building support teams, connecting with the world of feelings, removing blocks to progress, and tapping into the power of spirit through prayer and meditation.
 
The Tribe delivers all this support through formal processes that the Tribe members learn and practice at Tribe meetings.


Kind of Like a Cult


Tribe resembles a cult as it has some quirky customs, practices, and phrases. However, it does not conduct secret rituals, seek to convert people, make devotees swear eternal allegiance to a leader, or encourage members to harm themselves or surrender their property.


Kind of Like a University


Tribe resembles a university as people learn things, share their discoveries, and inspire each other. However, it does not have a standard curriculum, give grades, award a diploma, accept outside funding, or promote a political agenda.


Kind of Like a School of Fish


  











































A School of Fish
Source: https://www.sciencefocus.com/nature/how-do-schools-of-fish-swim-in-perfect-unison

Tribe resembles a school of fish as the Tribe members learn to move in harmony and to align with the flow of the larger society.


Schools of fish can move together as one collective organism, creating intricate and beautiful patterns as the individual fish synchronize their movements, almost simultaneously, as if dancing to the same spiritual music.  


Swarm scientists can model this choreography by simply endowing each fish with the awareness of six neighbors: above, below, left, right, front, and back.  Each fish uses its pressure-sensitive lateral lines and its vision to detect proximity and changes in proximity. Each fish swims in the center of a cube with its neighbors on the six faces. This model gives the entire school collective intelligence and each fish spiritual awareness of all the other fish.


In similar manner, during Tribe processes, a Tribe of eight members can surround one Member (Hotseat) with six other members and one extra to manage the process. 


Mankind naturally organizes itself into small, intimate Tribes, with each person a member of several Tribes: family, friends, interest groups, bridge groups, music jam groups, Bible study groups, etc. In this way, each person has spiritual awareness of the society’s collective intelligence.

Kind of Like Meditation


Tribe resembles meditation as the members engage in drumming and attain peaceful, blissful states. However, members can get quite noisy during role-playing as they encourage the Member on the hot seat to develop his forms.  


* Form: An outward physical display of inner emotion, such as squinting the eyes, rubbing the forehead, yawning, tapping a foot, or pounding an armrest.
* Hot seat: The members of the Trading Tribe trade turns helping each other locate and dissolve their issues and proceed toward serving others. The Tribe Member currently receiving attention metaphorically sits on a hot seat as he comes to notice and confront deep issues. Tribe members refer to the person on the hot seat as Hotseat. The Tribe Chief selects one Member as Hotseat to engage processes that may last between two minutes and two hours. The other members form the Healing Field of Acknowledgement and participate in role-playing and encouraging Hotseat to get past his defenses and dissolve his judgments.
* Role-Playing: The Chief’s deputy, the Process Manager, appoints members to take various roles in reenacting a critical event in Hotseat’s life. They keep rehearsing the play until Hotseat tells them they have it right. After Hotseat implements new proactive responses, something shifts, and the role-players find they can no longer run the drama.

Kind of Like Family


Tribe resembles family as the members come to know each other intimately, support each other, and know exactly how to push each other’s buttons.  However, the members refrain from trying to control or fix each other without invitation and take full responsibility for their own results.


Kind of Like a Social Group
Tribe resembles a social group as it provides a place for people to meet each other and form friendships. However, the friends go the extra mile and hold each other accountable for spiritual and personal growth.


Kind of Like an Interest Group


Tribe resembles an interest group such as a bridge club, poker group, book club, or jam circle as it provides regular association with friends with similar interests. However, it goes beyond casual social contact as members commit to actively supporting each other in working through their issues and learning to serve others.


Exactly Like Itself


Tribe has its own culture that includes specific notions, customs, and processes that appear later in this book.

An individual Tribe of about eight people forms an intentional community in which the members discover ways to experience themselves and to serve others. They help each other work through issues, dissolve judgments, realize goals, grow personally, have better relationships, and align their lives with service.  


Members take pride in their work and excel as employees, employers, entrepreneurs, parents, and friends. They see problems as opportunities. They release medicinal drama and respond proactively to situations.


* Proactive: A proactive response to a situation deals directly with the situation, not just with the symptoms.
* Medicinal: A medicinal response to a situation reduces feelings about a situation, diverts attention away from both the feelings and the situation, and supports procrastination.

A Tribe meeting feels like, well, nothing else. You sit in a circle with a bunch of friends who accept you completely and who encourage you to feel, express, and rehabilitate your feelings. In the process you feel your judgments melt away as your feelings change from adversaries to allies, and you find you lose your desire to attract drama. You get to participate in helping other Tribe members with their personal growth. You find yourself taking all these skills and sense of purpose out into the rest of your life. Simply, nothing feels quite like a Tribe meeting.  


To learn more about The Trading Tribe, you can read this book, confirm your understanding of how the Tribe works with the online comprehension tests, and ask the on-line Chief specific questions. After that, you might like to actually participate in Tribe and find out how it works in your own life.  


Tribe Size Matters


The Trading Tribe hosts a network of individual Tribes of about eight members each.  


Group intimacy depends on each Member maintaining a direct emotional connection with all the other members in the group, so we like small groups. Meanwhile, richness depends on the number of different points of view the members hold, so we like large groups.


In my experience, an eight-member group expresses the sweet spot that balances intimacy with richness. Below four members, a group loses richness of experience. Above twelve members, a group loses group intimacy.


My experience with Bible-study groups: They start out with about four people who soon form deep and nourishing emotional bonds with each other, support each other, and hold each other accountable. The word gets out about this magical group, and more people want to join. 


When the group size gets to about twelve people, it starts to lose intimacy. The leader of the group typically wants the group to grow, although he finds himself gradually changing roles from group moderator to instructor—from holding the space to delivering sermons. 


When the group size gets to about eighteen people, you might as well have the group move back into the chapel where the members can sit in rows and listen to the religious leader preach.


In Tribe we purposefully limit group size to about eight members who agree to stay the course for at least ten sessions.

Origins of the Tribe: The Whipsaw


  

























Whipsaw:  A long thin saw with a handle on both ends 
that two men use to drag back and forth across a log.  
In trading, a whipsaw references a rapid back-and-forth change in the price of a stock 
that motivates a trader to panic and take a loss.  Source: Wikipedia


Terry trades stocks. Tuesday afternoon, Terry buys a hundred shares of Digital Dumplings (a fictitious stock with symbol DD) at 50, planning to sell at 60. He calls his friend Jim to tell him all about it.


The stock, however, goes the other way and starts falling: 49, 48, 47. Terry feels surprise, anger, fear, embarrassment, irritation, and entrapment. 


DD keeps falling: 46, 45, 44. Terry can’t stand the sick feeling in his stomach any longer. He wants out now. He medicates his feeling by entering a sell order at the market and gets a fill at 43 for a loss of 14%.


He sees it fall even further to 42 and he feels a moment of relief. Then, right on cue, DD starts rising: 44, 45, 46. then leaping higher: 47, 51, 57, 63.  

He pounds his fist on his desk. “I can’t believe this. I keep doing this over and over!” he fumes to himself as he shakes his head, grinds his teeth, and squints his eyes and presses them into the heels of his hands. 


The next morning, Jim drops by. “Hey Terry,” he says, cheerfully patting him on the back, unaware of Terry’s loss. “Thank you for that great tip on DD. I owe you a lunch. Let me know if you see anything else.” 


“Yeah, right,” Terry replies as his stomach tightens. “Anything for a friend.”


“OK, I really have to tell you the details,” Jim continues with a Cheshire-cat grin. “After you recommend DD, I go back to my office and pick up a hundred shares at 47. After a little dip to 42, it just catches fire. I wind up selling it out this morning at 94 for a $4,700 profit. I don’t know how you do it, Terry. You sure know how to pick ’em. You definitely have the touch!”


So here we have it. Two traders, Terry and Jim, have the same idea about buying a stock, and both buy it within minutes on Tuesday afternoon. Terry panics and takes a whipsaw loss. Jim holds on and makes a tidy profit.


This kind of thing happens over and over and over. Different traders get different results, even with the same information. The difference has to do with how Terry and Jim manage their feelings. And although this story features two traders, it also applies to people from all walks of life in all kinds of situations, especially ones involving relationships. Click to play these trading songs:  The Whipsaw Song and The Big One That Got Away.


For many years, I watch myself and other traders struggle with our feelings. I notice that aside from complaining to each other about doing the same thing over and over, we don’t really have much of a clue about what to do or have a reliable support system for working through our issues.

In April 1992, I publish an article in Technical Tools Journal with the title “Lonely Traders” in which I report on the inherent emotional separateness of traders. I note they don’t connect emotionally about their trading; they don’t discuss it with their bosses, their customers, their wives, or even each other. I go on to suggest a way for traders to restructure their relationships in a way that nourishes their emotions and converts their feelings from adversaries into allies. 


I close with “If you feel your heart beating right about now, you might just feel me out here, beating the drum, calling the Tribe.” I call the group The Trading Tribe, a more manly-man moniker than the somewhat squishy appellation, Emotional Support Group for Traders.


A few weeks later a couple traders show up at my office in Los Altos, California, to ask me how to join the Trading Tribe. In amazement, yet without missing a beat, I tell them to show up at my home on Thursday evening and to bring some friends. And so starts the first Trading Tribe meeting.


In the fall of 1992, I publish another article in Technical Tools Journal with the title “Tribal Trading – Investing One Another” (not “investing in”—see box below) in which I report on our experiment in Tribal Trading. “We attribute much of our success to rediscovering emotional and spiritual resources available through the mutual support, encouragement, and multilevel sharing of the Tribe.” More people inquire about the Tribe and some join.

The Trading Tribe continues to meet weekly at my home in Los Altos Hills. We talk about our trading and our feelings about trading. As we go along, we develop new ways to hold hope, fear, greed, despair, and a whole closet full of emotional ghosts and goblins waiting to come out to sabotage the best of our plans.  


As we continue to meet week after week, we develop formal methods that focus our attention on the discovery and resolution of issues such as how to recover emotionally after taking a series of whipsaw losses, only to go on to miss the major move. 


I call this collection of methods the Trading Tribe Process or TTP©. Through trial and error, research and inspiration, TTP© comes to include a rich set of processes, customs, and notions and a supporting philosophy. 








TTP(c)
The Trading Tribe Process

for rehabilitating our feelings
and realigning ourselves toward serving others
	



To our delight, the Trading Tribe Process actually works! We develop better relationships with our feelings and our trading improves measurably. Surprisingly, we also get better results in our relationships with our friends, spouses, children, and associates.


The Tribe grows and other chapters spring up. Hundreds of people from all over the world report gains in all kinds of areas of their lives from the Trading Tribe Process. People show up and help each other identify their symptoms and the issues behind the symptoms; they dissolve their issues and stop attracting similar ones.


Trading issues resolve surprisingly quickly; traders either systematize their trading or they stop trading. Either way, they soon stop using trading as a medicinal distraction from deeper issues and turn their interest to interpersonal and spiritual issues and to serving others. Members report less drama and more success in their personal relationships with their family members, colleagues, employees, employers, associates, and friends. They also report feeling less lonely and thinking of their work less as a job and more as a career.


For the next couple of decades, I continue hosting Trading Tribe meetings and honing the Trading Tribe Process through trial and error and reflection. I write a book, The Trading Tribe, the precursor to this book, that shows people how to set up and run their own Tribes. I offer workshops for people who wish to lead Tribes. I set up a blog for people to share their Trading Tribe experiences and inspire others. Tribes spring up all over the world. The Tribe evolves to include a formal foundation of notions, customs, and processes.


At one point, just prior to the appearance of COVID, I count somewhere around thirty-five Trading Tribes operating all around the world. The members learn how to change their basic attitudes, response patterns, and even personalities; they get rapid and lasting results.


After each Tribe meeting, each Member writes a report about his experience for posting to the anonymous FAQ blog to inspire members of other Tribes. In the early stages, I read and comment on each report and post it online. As the Tribe grows, so does the administrative overload. I fall behind in my postings. I find myself bottlenecking my own organization. 


Then, COVID hits. People no longer travel to Tribe meetings to sit in a circle and look at other people wearing masks. Some people try conducting meetings online; that withers away for lack of an on-line protocol to compensate for lack of direct personal contact. The Trading Tribe sputters to a halt.


I take that as an opportunity to take a break, meditate, ponder, evaluate, cogitate, consolidate, revise and extend my vision for The Trading Tribe. In this book I call the Tribe together again to follow the vision to the next level. 


First, I fire myself as the administrative bottleneck; I replace myself with this website that administers the Tribe. Members can use this site to find a Tribe, join a Tribe, start a Tribe, mentor a Tribe, and document their experiences in the Trading Tribe Knowledge Base. They can also pose questions about the Tribe with my digital clone, the Chief. Tribe members may still write directly to me for comment and posting to FAQ, although this no longer has an effect on the flow of the Tribe.

How to Learn TTP(C)


Learning the Trading Tribe Process (TTP©) has some similarities with learning to ride a bicycle. You can read all about bicycle theory and watch people ride bicycles and still not know how to ride one. You can even get on a bicycle and wobble around and fall off and still not know.  


If you keep at it, and especially if you have someone to guide you and if you also have other bicycle riders to inspire you, you can fairly quickly get a feel for it. Soon, the wobbles disappear, and you find yourself cruising along without even thinking about how you do it.  


You may not even consciously notice that at low speeds you push on the right handlebar to turn left, and at high speeds you push on the right handlebar to turn right. You just instinctively do it. If you don’t believe this, try it out yourself or ask someone who races bicycles or motorcycles about the gyroscopic effect of rapidly spinning wheels. TTP© installs upgrades to our subconscious response patterns on similar automatic, instinctive, subconscious levels.


This book tells you all about TTP© theory. It also shows you how Tribe members practice TTP© and how they conduct many of the processes. If you keep at it, join a Tribe, and participate fully, the whole picture comes into view, you have an aha moment, and you just know how to do it. Sort of like riding a bicycle.

Notions, Customs, and Methods

The Trading Tribe has its own philosophy, including notions, customs, and processes. In this chapter I condense the Trading Tribe philosophy into an overview.
Notions


* Feelings: All feelings have positive intentions. Judging feelings ties them in knots, reduces their usefulness, and contributes to confusion and drama. Trading Tribe processes help people untie their judgment knots, reactivate their feelings, and reclaim their power. People have automatic responses to their feelings; we call these response patterns “rocks.” Trading Tribe processes can help replace medicinal rocks that medicate feelings with proactive rocks that move us forward.  


* Now: All events happen in the ever-evolving moment of now. We cannot access or change the past or future that exist only as concepts in the ever-evolving moment of now.  


* Feedback: In Tribe we model behavior as the ongoing interaction of elements in feedback systems. The structure (intention) of a system determines its behavior (results), or, as we say in Tribe, Intentions = Results. The alternative, causal model, does not explain behavior; it does, however, support notions of guilt and blame and our modern legal and political systems.


* Community: We derive our identity and purpose through community. The Trading Tribe, an intentional community, aims to support its members in working through issues and finding purpose in sharing gifts and serving others. We cannot satisfy the feeling of emotional emptiness with acquisitions; we satisfy it by trading what we already have with others.


* Spirituality: We all connect to each other on a spiritual plane. In Tribe we acknowledge, celebrate, and incorporate these connections in our own lives.


Customs


* Listening: We actively listen to each other and treat others’ communications as precious gifts. We do not try to sell our points of view or try to win arguments.


* Acceptance: We accept each other fully and without judgment. We provide a Healing Field of Acknowledgment for each other during processes.


* Accountability: We hold ourselves and each other accountable for keeping promises and realizing our potentials.


* Language: We use clear language to support clear thinking. We use SVO-p syntax (Subject, Verb, Object, present tense). We also avoid using forms of the verb to be and use descriptive, sensual verbs instead.


* Willingness: We respect each other’s willingness; we frequently test for willingness during processes. In case a Tribe Member expresses unwillingness to proceed, we terminate the process and pass on to the next willing Member.








Methods


* Processes: The Trading Tribe uses formal processes to work on ourselves and come to serve others.

Community


Life happens in community—birds in flocks, bees in swarms, cattle in herds, ants in mounds, lions in prides. Without community the individual has little identity, meaning, purpose, or even life-support system. A lone bird, bee, cow, ant, or lion does not survive for long.


The same holds for people. People need other people. A person may belong to several communities (e.g., family, work, church, bridge club). If he loses one of his minor communities, he misses it. If he loses one of his major ones, he may lose his identity, his desire to serve others, his standing, and his physical, mental, and emotional connections. He may wind up in deep depression. Communities define us and give our lives meaning.


We depend on community. People in community support each other in dealing with issues. The members of the Trading Tribe come together to trade turns helping each other dissolve our judgments, rehabilitate our feelings, and come to better serve others.


Ad-Hoc Communities

People who have common interests (such as wanting to fix the potholes over on 3rd Street) tend to form ad-hoc communities. They rarely have a conscious philosophy or behavioral guidelines or a reason to continue once they accomplish their mission.


Intentional Community


An intentional community has a definite long-term purpose, beliefs, procedures, and qualifications. It also has methods to keep the focus on the purpose.


Service Community


Service communities dedicate to serving others. People who participate in service communities generally enjoy the feeling of the lift of a driving dream.


Vicky, Sherry, and Nancy get together once a week to volunteer at their church. They provide meals, hot showers, and fresh clothes for homeless people. 


In addition, they go the extra mile and get to know their clients personally. They all sit together at long tables and commune as they break bread and connect heart-to-heart. They feel the glow that comes from helping others and by receiving help. Some of the clients even wind up attending the church, getting jobs, and coming back to serve newcomers. 


Across town, in a government agency, the administrator of the homeless-assistance program distributes checks with little human, heart-to-heart connection. No rapport, no community, no glow. The difference between the three women and the administrator: heart-to-heart, intimate connection.


Longing for Community


A century ago, people live in a more agrarian culture and in smaller towns, and they rely on community for identity, meaning, and support. They have town-hall meetings to discuss building a new school or fixing potholes or finding ways to take care of Old George, who “just ain’t right since he fall out the ol’ oak tree.”


Lately, with the centralization and concentration of public assistance into large, impersonal agencies, we delegate civic matters to representatives, and we no longer deal with community issues intimately in community with each other.


We lose our direct emotional connection with our peers and with those we aim to help. No one feels the glow. How The Trading Tribe Works


A Trading Tribe has about eight members and meets every two to three weeks for a six-month, ten-meeting series. A meeting lasts about six hours and includes various processes and also includes breaking bread together. A Tribe may have all men, all women, or a mixture.


Tribe meetings follow a protocol. At first, new members may find the protocol cumbersome and overly formal; after they master it, they come to see it as highly efficient as it eliminates distraction and greatly improves the quality of the meetings. 


Applicants commit to attending all ten meetings of a series. At the end of a ten-meeting series, the Tribe Chief may announce another series. Some members may leave and make room for others to join. Others may carry on for another series. At the end of a series, members may sign up for another series with the same Chief or try a different Tribe.


A Member may also start his own Tribe by qualifying and getting a mentor. Members on travels may also pollinate between Tribes to learn and share techniques.


The Trading Tribe does not charge members for attending meetings.  Members pay for an annual membership to gain access to all Tribe resources. 


The local Tribe Chief hosts the meetings, provides the drums, and arranges the meal; the other members may also bring their own drums and offer to chip in for the meal. Otherwise, and by design, money does not change hands between members.


Nor can you buy your way in to the Trading Tribe; you have to work your way in by mastering the material and by continuing to demonstrate willingness to trade turns supporting each other.


To join, simply follow the instructions on the website. First you read this book and learn how the Tribe works. Then you take a basic comprehension test; you may keep taking it until you get 100% on it. When you qualify, you may apply for membership in a local Tribe or for a mentor if you wish to start a Tribe of your own. Your mentor, a Member with Tribe experience personally guides you while you “learn the ropes.” 


I may also present an occasional optional weekend training for Tribe leaders. It typically starts on Friday at 6:00 pm and runs until Sunday at noon. You may join or even start a Tribe without attending a workshop. Everything you need appears in this book.


During Tribe meetings, the members sit in a circle while the Chief conducts various processes that guide the members in supporting one another in addressing their issues. A process may run between five minutes and two hours. 


The Chief selects a Member with an issue and a strong desire to work on the hot seat (the Member actually stays in the same physical chair he already occupies). The Tribe refers to this Member as Hotseat and then supports him by encouraging him to stay on track with the processes. The members provide the Healing Field of Acknowledgment to support Hotseat’s diving ever deeper into his forms and also by taking part in role-playing.  


Most people spend their lives solving the same old problem over and over. Listen to the song: Jumping Off This Train. In Tribe, Hotseat comes to understand the mechanisms by which he attracts, executes, and institutionalizes his same old problems. During the processes he typically experiences aha moments in which he releases the judgment that underpins the need to continue solving a particular problem; the problem then simply dissolves. People generally do not believe this possible until they observe it happening in their fellow Tribe members and in themselves.


Hotseat learns how to reprogram his subconscious so he radiates clear intentions and attracts success effortlessly just in the process of living. He comes to the hot seat to solve a problem and he winds up dissolving it. He also comes to turn his interest away from medicinal drama and toward proactively serving others.


Concurrently, the other members experience Hotseat’s issue from their points of view and may dissolve issues of their own. At the end of a process, all members check out by sharing their experiences of the process. The Chief may then call for a short break or for the meal. 


The meal provides an opportunity for the members to get to know each other informally over a meal, further strengthening connections between the members of the Tribe community.


After the break, the Tribe reconvenes in a circle. The Chief surveys the members to identify the next Member currently most willing to take the hot seat.

The Same Old Problem


Cynthia marries Sam (her third, his second) and almost immediately discovers problems with the marriage. She spends a year trying to save her marriage, another year in counseling, and another year getting another divorce. After a year of grieving and more therapy, she meets Jim, the answer to her problem at last. Soon, they marry. Almost immediately, she discovers problems. Cynthia keeps solving the same old problem over and over.


Ernie trades the futures markets. He starts out with a small position and makes a little money. He feels confident, so he doubles his position and makes even more money. He feels no fear, except the fear of missing out. He doubles up again. The market stalls, then starts to rise again. Ernie does not want to miss out on the ride of a lifetime. He goes all in, right on the close on Friday, at the high of the move.


Next Monday, the market begins to fade. Tuesday and Wednesday it continues to sag. On Thursday, Ernie gets a margin call. He can’t meet it and the brokerage house sells him out close to the bottom of the correction, leaving him with no positions and about a quarter of his original investment.  Two months later, he opens another account with another broker, finds another play, starts to make money, doubles up, doubles up again, and wipes out on a margin call. Ernie keeps solving the same old problem over and over.


Chico attends a support group for alcoholics at his church. This week he gets his one-year sobriety chip after spending a year sober, transforming his appearance and getting a new girlfriend, a new job, new friends, and self respect. On the way to the ceremony he hardly believes he deserves it. In a moment of inspiration, he stops at The Thirteenth Step, a bar on 6th Street, for a tiny little reward to himself for his accomplishment. Then another and another. He misses the ceremony. Chico keeps solving the same problem over and over.


Most of us spend our lives solving the same old problems over and over—falling into the causal model with a partner and engaging unending conflict about whom to blame, endlessly searching for the ideal mate, feeling stuck in a job, feeling undervalued, not having enough money, struggling to lose weight, not feeling our own lives matter, dealing with the feeling of something missing.


Even more, we don’t even work on original problems. We inherit our same old problems from our parents and other role models. We continue to keep the family tradition alive by recycling our same old problems over and over, at least until we can teach them to our own children.


We keep attracting and solving the same old problems, and these problems all have something in common: they all revolve around trying to avoid some feeling or another.  


For example, Ernie does not like the feeling of fear. As a child, smaller than others in his class, he attracts bullies who periodically beat him up and dunk his head in a swirling toilet. He lives in fear. He associates fear with bullies and with his own physical pain. He does not like the pain, he does not like the bullies, and, by association, he does not like fear.


Ernie has his fear in a knot. A knot ties two elements together: (1) a feeling and (2) a judgment about that feeling.


Let’s see how Ernie’s knot attracts his problems. The feeling, fear, has a positive intention to inform us about risk. Fear normally motivates us to avoid or move away from high-risk situations. Ernie, with his fear in a knot, does not feel much fear, so he rarely implements the positive intention of fear. Fearless Ernie approaches the bullies and may even tease them a bit just to get things moving. Sure enough, he soon winds up receiving another “swirly.”


Ernie has the same old problem in other areas such as with the markets. He ignores his fear. He keeps buying more, almost teasing the markets and daring them to react. Soon enough, Ernie gets the same old result.


Trying to deal with the bullies or trying to deal with the markets does not solve any problems. Indeed, attempts to solve a problem merely motivate the dynamic through yet another cycle.  




How to Solve
the Same Old Problem 


Stop solving the problem and
start dissolving knots.
	

We cannot solve our same old problem. We can, however, dissolve it by untying its core knots, liberating our feelings, and then responding to them with proactive response patterns. I call feeling-to-action response patterns “rocks.” Medicinal rocks help us medicate and suppress our feelings; proactive rocks deal with situations directly and effectively. In Tribe meetings we use physical stones to represent our rocks during role-playing.


Our knots do not untie themselves. Indeed, they protect themselves. If we try to untie one of our own knots, our subconscious puts us to sleep or distracts us by directing us to go to the refrigerator for a snack or to start a fight with a spouse.  


Nor do we automatically acquire proactive rocks. We have longstanding comfortable relationships with our medicinal rocks; we adopt them early in our lives to medicate our trauma and pain, and we bury them deeply and do not replace them easily.


Nor do we consciously understand the knots and rocks that run our lives. They do not live in the mind; they live deep in the gut and emerge only briefly to help us avoid pain.  


As such, advice and one-on-one talk therapy may have little effect on them.


We can, however, cut through the protective layers by forming an intentional community and placing Hotseat directly into the Healing Field of Acknowledgement. 


As the community relentlessly cheers him on, Hotseat descends through his judgments and inhibitions into his raw feelings. He comes to know all about the critical incidents in which he creates his knots and rocks. Once Hotseat sees how the dots connect, he can follow one or another of the Trading Tribe processes to untie his knots and replace his medicinal rocks with proactive rocks.   


When he succeeds, he loses interest in solving his same old problems, and he naturally moves into alignment with serving others. When he releases his issues and his need for medicinal drama, he naturally comes into spiritual alignment with his gifts and begins sharing them with others just in the process of life itself. He lives effortlessly and joyously. Although others notice a change in him, he may not notice much change in himself; he more likely notices everyone else changing.


Ultimately, the best gifts we can give each other and the most loving acts we can perform all begin with helping each other get free from our judgment knots, implement proactive rocks, and then exercise the freedom to connect with ourselves and with each other in joyous expression of serving others.


The beautiful part of this gift: we all get to build it together in community. 


These processes generally align with, support, and accelerate conventional psychotherapy. As with therapy, the processes work if you have a way to maintain focus, stay on task, and not meander off into comfortable conversation.


To join and remain in the Trading Tribe, the members have to continue demonstrating willingness to participate fully, to work on themselves and on each other, and to take their services out into their larger communities to help others. This comprises the ongoing work of the Tribe.

Essential Tribe Notions


The Trading Tribe members share some essential notions about feelings, the moment of now, feedback, community, and spirituality. I make no claim for the truth of these notions; however, I notice that a Tribe that embraces them during Tribe meetings seems to work better.
Tribe Notion #1: Emotions and Feelings
Psychologists and therapists study and assist clients with emotions and feelings. 


Surprisingly, they do not agree on the basic definitions of feelings and emotions. Some use the terms interchangeably. Check the internet. Some say we have 34,000 basic emotions; others say 87, 27, 12, 9, 8, 6, 5, and 4.  


Some say feelings have higher or lower tones and that we can rank them. Some call some feelings virtuous and other feelings sinful. Some say physical feelings such as hunger and thirst differ fundamentally from emotions such as anger and sadness. Some put names on emotions and arrange them into a wheel of emotions. Others put similar names into a wheel of feelings. Some use emotions, feelings, conditions, properties, and judgments interchangeably.


Some think that we have “good” and “bad” feelings and that we can work to get rid of the bad ones and get more of the good ones. Some think some feelings derive from other feelings. Some think we can come to control our feelings; others propose to help us express or overcome our feelings.

Some people use the terms emotions and feelings interchangeably; in Tribe we draw sharp distinctions. 




Emotions: In Tribe “emotion” refers to an automatic reaction to external stimuli. If you see a bright light or hear a baby cry or have a problem, you generate an emotion. All emotions have positive intentions: namely, to help us respond quickly and decisively to problems. People have difficulty defining emotions, perhaps since definitions reside in the conscious mind while emotions reside in the subconscious mind, and these realms do not directly connect.


Feelings: In Tribe “feelings” provide a bridge between subconscious emotions and conscious thoughts. They bring some emotions to conscious awareness and allow us to respond to situations logically.  


Knots: If we have a judgment about a feeling, we have that feeling in a judgment knot. The knot ties up the feeling and prevents us from responding proactively to the feeling; we may wind up medicating the feeling instead.


For example, anger normally helps us identify and remedy boundary violations. Someone who carries a judgment about anger has an anger knot. He does not like to feel anger; instead of dealing with a boundary violation, he turns to medicinal remedies such as drugs, alcohol, and distractive drama to try to make the feeling of anger go away.  


People with anger knots tend to fall prey to predators. People with sadness knots tend to lose things. People with jealousy knots tend to attract unfaithful partners. The appearance of jealousy early in the relationship tends to flag infidelity; it serves as a warning not to engage the relationship in the first place. 


The Tribe has many processes for identifying judgment, untying judgment knots, and rehabilitating our feelings.

How Many Feelings


We have no definitive consensus about how many feelings humans can experience or that people experience them in the same way. Most people recognize some feeling names such as joy, sadness, fear, anger, disgust, and surprise. 


Rocks 


In Tribe a rock refers to the function that converts a feeling to an external action response; rocks determine what we do in response to our feelings.  Proactive rocks guide us to respond proactively to our feelings and to solve problems. Medicinal rocks guide us to medicate the feeling that attends the emotion without addressing the underlying situation. In Tribe we have processes for identifying rocks and for upgrading them. Our rocks determine how we respond to our feelings and thus also determine our personalities and our fortunes.


Service, Emotions, and Feelings


People who devote to serving others enjoy an overall sense of emotional fulfillment and satisfaction. Absent serving others, they may experience meaninglessness and emptiness and turn to medicinal remedies to deal with the loneliness and lack of purposes. Serving others supports willingness and acceptance and helps prevent some forms of addiction.  

Forms refer to involuntary corporal reactions to emotions like squinting the eyes in sunlight, lactating in response to a baby crying, and puckering the lips when tasting lemon juice. Forms physically manifest and communicate our emotions and feelings to others. 


In Tribe we do not attempt to categorize or even name emotions and feelings as we have no reliable way to detect them or work with them. We can, however, focus on forms. This bypasses the futility of defining emotions and feelings and focuses our attention on the forms that we can actually use in processes.


The subconscious mind and the conscious mind communicate with each other using two important languages: feelings and forms. 


If you smell vinegar, you have an emotional response that generates a form (squinting the eyes and wrinkling the nose) and a feeling (a pungent smell).  The conscious mind understands both these languages. In this way, the subconscious mind informs the conscious mind about emotions.


You can also consciously generate a body form by squinting your eyes. You can consciously generate a feeling by imagining smelling vinegar.
Feelings and body forms connect the conscious and subconscious minds. They inform our conscious minds about our emotions; in Tribe they also provide a handle on the subconscious that we can use to reprogram our subconscious responses.  


Body forms appear when a problem excites an emotion. Forms show up as posture, facial expression, and body motions. Vincent recalls an incident at work and purses his lips, squeezes his eyes shut, wrinkles his nose, and jiggles his left leg. Those expressions transmit Vincent’s emotions to the other Tribe members. The members do not have to understand or even name Vincent’s situation, emotions, or feelings. He transmits that information through his forms.


If the other members wish to encourage Vincent to go deep and explore his feelings, they do not have to know what he feels; they can simply encourage him to amplify his forms. “Good work, Vincent! Keep squinting your eyes shut and wrinkle your nose more and keep jiggling your left leg.”


A form registers body strain associating with internal emotional stress. A Member may express clenching of the teeth, squinting of the eyes, and clenching of the fists in association with anger. In Tribe we do not work with emotions or feelings directly as we cannot access them directly; we work with forms that we can see, measure, and encourage.


We can easily read Vincent’s forms and get a physical sense of his emotional state. We can also help him go deeper into his emotions by encouraging him to amplify his forms. If we try to tell him to amplify an emotion, such as fear, we risk pulling him out of his feelings and into his conscious mind, thus ending the subconscious process.

Tribe Notion #4: Causality / Feedback Systems


When something happens, people generally want to know why it happens. They naturally look for a simple cause-and-effect relationship. If they can’t identify a simple cause, they may look for something more complex, such as a causal chain or a common cause or a common effect.


  

Curiously, while these simple models might help explain very simple events, such as how the cue ball sinks the eight ball in the corner pocket, these causal models do not apply very well to the real world of people and relationships. 

In the real world, we get a better understanding of dynamic systems by noticing everything has some effect on everything else through delays and feedback.


Feedback loops offer a much better way to model and understand the complex workings of dynamic systems, including relationships.


  

















In the real world, whenever we have A causing B, we also have B causing A in a feedback loop. For example, Art thinks that Beth gives him a disapproving look, so he gives her a defiant look in return. After a few more back-and-forth, increasingly critical looks, they start to exchange insults and then threats. Soon, a physical fight erupts. The fight has no single cause.  Both Art and Beth take turns escalating the looks into insults and eventually physical violence.  


After the fight, they continue to argue about whom to blame for starting it.  Causality does not help understand the situation; if anything, it provides ammunition to prolong it as Art and Beth take turns blaming each other as the cause.


A much more useful explanation for the fight lies in noticing the ways both Art and Beth respond to each other in a feedback loop as they take turns escalating the conflict.
 
As long as Art and Beth stay with the causal model, they continue to blame each other; when they switch into feedback-system thinking, they no longer consider blame and guilt; instead, they notice how they respond to each other, and they may even start to come up with ways to support rather than attack each other.


Statisticians typically deduce cause by looking for local relationships that happen in the same geographical area and temporal relationships that happen in the same moment. Forcing behavior into causal models can lead to bizarre conclusions such as “ice-cream cones cause sunburns.” 

Positive Feedback Loops

When people respond to each other in an escalating manner, the system contains a positive feedback loop. Positive refers to the positive reinforcement of the conflict, not to the “negative” things people might say to each other.


Positive feedback explains growth. In a savings account, the more money in the account, the more interest it pays and the faster the account grows. In a functional relationship, the partners take turns building each other up and making each other stronger and happier. A couple of duckweed in a pond double every day or two and soon cover the pond.


Negative Feedback Loops


Negative feedback loops help us achieve goals.  


Your home thermostat uses negative feedback to control the temperature.










  







































































Image Source: www.Carrier.com 


Goal-Seeking Negative-Feedback Loop


Say we set the thermostat to 72 degrees (the goal). If the room temperature falls to 71 degrees, the thermostat senses a difference (or problem) of 1 degree and turns on the furnace. The room temperature rises to 72 degrees, the difference falls to 0 degrees, and the thermostat turns off the furnace.   


When the temperature goes below the set point, the thermostat turns the furnace on. When the room temperature goes above the set point, the thermostat turns on the A/C. In this way, the thermostat system keeps the room temperature in a narrow range around the set point. 


Self-regulating systems that seek a goal contain a negative feedback loop that uses the difference between the goal and the current state of the system to apply corrective action to change the state of the system. Engineers refer to this difference as the error. The word “negative” comes from the subtraction of the state from the goal to get the error. Negative feedback keeps moving the actual state toward the goal. Negative feedback systems that contain delay may display overshoot and oscillation.  


Charlie jumps in the shower and wants the water a little hotter. He turns the handle toward hot. At first, nothing happens as the water takes a while to arrive at the shower head (the delay). Meanwhile Charlie keeps moving the handle toward hot. Presently, the hot water arrives and Charlie has to turn the handle toward cold. After another delay, he fine-tunes the temperature to “just right.”


Such oscillations gradually die out. Servo (feedback systems) engineers call this die-out phenomenon “damping.” Damping shows up in stock prices as they seek a new level and form a “wedge” pattern, in the motion of a key as a drunk tries to get it in the keyhole, in pendulums, and above all in banjo strings after you pluck ’em.

The operation of feedback systems may appear counter-causal and counter-intuitive.


Feedback System Curiosities


1. You can make the living room temperature rise by putting a glass of ice water on top of the thermostat.


2. If you have an electric blanket with separate temperature controls, and you switch the controls, one person winds up freezing while the other fries.


3. Rent control leads to housing shortages.


Intentions = Results


The structure of a system (its intention) generates its behavior (its results). If you want a different behavior, you have to change the structure of the system to get it. Your results reflect your intentions, so Intentions = Results. We can measure our intentions by the results we get.  


While we don’t always get the results we think we intend, we do always get the results we actually intend, including saying we don’t want the results we get. In this way, everyone gets what they want.




Everyone Gets What They Want,


including getting to pretend
that they don’t.
	



The notion of intentions = results supports the practice of taking full responsibility for our lives.  


People operating out of the causal model might interpret intentions = results as a form of blaming the victim. The causal model enables blame. In the system model, we have no causal linkages; hence, no guilt or blame. 


For example, a university student claims he wants to pass an exam and says he has the intention to pass. He hires a tutor and studies every night for a week before the exam. His result: he fails the exam. By intentions = results, we have his intention to fail. 


Upon reflection, we might notice that studying every night does not ensure passing. An actual intention to pass might include hiring a tutor who uses frequent testing and adequate diagnostics to locate deficiencies and remedy them. Thus, the student would have practice passing the exam before taking it and easily pass.


In Tribe and through the Tribe processes, we come to see ourselves less as victims of a cause and more as participants in a co-creation.

The Business of Isness 
In Tribe we generally avoid using forms of the verb to be. Technically, a thing can be only itself; it cannot be anything else. Saying “John is smart” or “John is handsome” literally indicates John equals these adjectives. Actually, he does not equal these adjectives. He equals something else that happens to have those properties.  


This might seem like a needlessly nit-picky distinction. However, the subconscious mind responds very literally to our suggestions, so the effort to communicate precisely pays off in clarity of intention and speed of resolution of issues.


In Tribe we celebrate our commitment to clarity (and clear agreements) by using descriptive verbs that more precisely identify specific actions. To rephrase the above using descriptive verbs: John scores higher than anyone else in his class on the final physics exam; John receives an offer from a movie studio to star as the male lead in a romantic comedy. 


In Tribe we can extend SVOP syntax (Subject, Verb, Object, present-tense) by replacing all forms of the verb to be with action verbs, and wind up with SVOP-b syntax, an even higher standard for clarity of thought and communication. SVOP-b syntax motivates us to think about what we mean to say and then to find colorful, descriptive verbs that say it.

People starting to use SVOP-b syntax generally find it unnatural, cumbersome, and frustrating. With practice, the wobbles tend to smooth out and you wind up cruising along, thinking and communicating more clearly.  


In addition, the subconscious mind seems quite comfortable with SVOP-b syntax. Some of the Tribe processes put Hotseat and other members in a light trance, so using SVOP-b during Tribe meetings helps protect the communal trance.  


SVOP-b helps illuminate fuzzy thinking and writing. Authors of legal documents frequently omit the subject (the responsible party) and put actions in the past and in the future to avoid taking responsibility. Rewriting legal documents in SVOP-b identifies the responsible party and brings the action back into the present. 


Example of legalese:  “Failure to comply will not be tolerated.”


Here we have no subject, no object, future tense, the verb to be and then past tense all in one sentence. We do not know who does the tolerating, nor exactly when that person plans to do that something in the future in the past.  Such convolution does not make for clarity; it makes for large legal fees when parties to an agreement have inevitable conflicts and need an attorney to sort out the nonexisting meaning.


Occasionally, corporations that wish to do a deal find they have minor disagreement that prevents execution. If they cannot agree, they may resort to the ol’ 2.5-page maneuver. It works like this: They hire an attorney who can write a single 2.5-page long sentence that has no subject or object and that frequently switches back and forth between tenses and relies heavily on forms of the verb, “to be.” Of course, no one can understand the sentence, and, of most importance, they all know no one else understands it either. So they all sign it and the deal goes through. One side-effect: The attorney can thereafter earn a pretty good living for decades interpreting the sentence whenever a conflict arises.


For examples of SVOP-b, see the speeches of US President John Kennedy and the song lyrics of John Lennon, two great and clear communicators who show us how to reach people heart-to-heart. Listen to the song: You Move Me.

Some Relationship Customs


Own Feelings: We own our feelings and take responsibility for them; we do not blame our feelings on others. We do not guess what other people feel, we do not tell other people what they feel, and we do not judge others’ feelings; we simply ask how the other person feels and then listen. 


Ask Positively: We state what we desire positively and optimistically, without complaining, criticizing, threatening, nagging, or belittling.


Adult-to-Adult: We avoid relating to each other as children and parents. We relate to each other as adults.


Reliability: We clarify agreements before we make them. After we make agreements, we keep them or modify them only by mutual consent.


Health: We observe healthy practices in our diets, hygiene, exercise, sleep, and stress management.


Kindness: We practice kindness and compassion for others.


Imagination: We support each other in imagining and fantasizing. When we get stuck in a problem, we work to release the judgment about our feelings and let go of the problem. We then imagine a larger problem about how to serve others and move on to it.


The Work of Love: Loving means following all the customs above. This can take a lot of work. When we actually do the work of love, we get a big reward—namely, a warm-fuzzy loving feeling. The loving feeling comes from doing the work of love. When we do the work of love, we wind up with primary partners who listen to our feelings, hold us tight, and never leave us.

If we try to get the warm-fuzzy feeling of love without doing the work of love, we may wind up with the prickly feeling of longing instead. This can entrain manipulation, control, guilt, bullying, frustration, exhaustion, depression, making others responsible for our feelings, and writing country songs. Intimacy-Centric / Control-Centric


In Tribe we learn to balance intimacy-centric relating (sharing feelings) and control-centric relating (telling people what to do).  


Intimacy-Centric Communication




We express our feelings to each other directly and with respect, and we acknowledge others for expressing their feelings to us. We say things like “I feel warm,” “I want a chocolate ice cream cone,” and “I feel afraid of buying this stock.”   


Control-Centric Communication 


Control-centric communication revolves around trying to control situations. We say things like “You should keep your elbows off the table,” “You need to wash your hands,” and “Forceps, STAT!”


People also calculate the effect honesty might have on the other person and throttle their expression of feelings for fear of reprisal. Claudia, age 38, has a daughter, Nancy, age 2-1/2. Claudia sees an uncomfortable look on her daughter’s face and asks her if she has a wet diaper. Nancy looks at the floor and replies, “No, mama, still dry.” Claudia frowns and in a stiff tone demands, “Nancy, tell me the truth.” Nancy, with tears in her eyes, says, “A little bit wet.”








People Lie
to avoid reprisal.


If you stop judging others,
they start telling the truth.
	



So here we have it, Claudia teaches her daughter how to lie at age 2-1/2. To see how this works, notice Claudia operating in the control-centric model, telling her daughter that she has to learn how to use the toilet so that mommy doesn’t have to change her diapers so often. This makes the child responsible for the parent’s happiness. Nancy does not want to disappoint her mother, and she does not want to risk reprisals for having a wet diaper, so she makes a rational choice and lies.


Consider the alternative, intimacy-centric approach. Claudia tells her daughter her feelings. “Nancy, I can tell by your expression when you have a cold, wet diaper, and I can feel that cold, wet feeling too. I want to change that wet and cold diaper for a new warm and dry one. Also, I know a wet diaper can give you a rash, so I want to protect you with a new diaper. Some day, you can learn to use the toilet and not have to wear diapers at all. For now, we can work together on this. You can help mommy keep you safe and dry by telling me when you have a wet diaper.”


Nancy enjoys her mother’s sharing her feelings and feels safe and close to her mother. She feels her mother’s love in expressing her desire to keep her safe and dry. In the process, she learns to share her own feelings and knows she can please her mother by telling the truth about her feelings. Nancy stops lying and happily announces, “Mommy, I have a wet diaper.” Her mother responds, “Nancy, thank you for letting me know. Let’s get a nice dry one on you.” Both Claudia and Nancy come to practice intimacy-centric, emotionally-honest relating.


Of course, intimacy-centric relating might not come naturally for Claudia, and she might have to do some internal work to get herself into the intimacy-centric state. She attends a Tribe meeting and works through her judgments about changing diapers and also about having a happy, healthy daughter. She comes to view changing diapers as a way to serve her daughter and deepen her intimacy with her. She sees that her daughter makes a rational choice to lie and that by sharing honestly with her daughter she can remove her daughter’s incentive for lying and model emotional honesty.

Rapport, Tact, and Diplomacy
The Trading Tribe supports and encourages personal growth; it supports Hotseat in going deep into his feelings, dissolving his judgments, and changing how he responds to others. Now and again, the PM purposefully pushes Hotseat’s buttons to “light him up,” encourage him to experience his pain, and locate the feelings he has in judgment knots while the other members cheer him on. During these processes the PM frequently tests Hotseat for willingness to proceed. 


Tribe members come to enjoy the camaraderie of swimming around in the nonjudgmental growth cauldron as they help each other with the work of releasing judgment. In the process, they develop considerable skill in noticing other people’s thoughts, feelings, forms, judgments, and fuzzy thinking. 


They love the environment; they rarely find it anywhere else.


Outside of Tribe meetings, however, Tribe members must remember that their non-Tribe friends and associates have no agreements or expectations to follow Tribe notions and customs. They may not appreciate someone pointing out their fuzzy thinking or insisting they say things “correctly.” They almost certainly do not want someone purposefully pushing their buttons.


In the “real world,” members must pay extra attention to rapport, tact, and diplomacy in dealing with others; otherwise, they may drift into “fixing” the other person and putting him on the defensive.


To illustrate further, let’s go dancing.








  























https://www.businessinsider.com/what-morning-sober-rave-dancing-party-is-like-2017-2 




Bill and Wanda go out drinking and dancing. As they prepare to leave,  Wanda notices Bill still has a bit of a buzz on and asks Bill for the keys.


Wanda:  (control-centric) “Bill, give me the keys. I better drive.”


Wanda’s controlling tone pushes Bill’s buttons. He feels shame and resentment and medicates these feelings by attacking with fake-feelings mode. He starts his sentence with “I feel” and then goes on to judge Wanda rather than express a feeling.


Bill (fake-feeling defensive attack): “I feel you always overreact.”


Wanda (even more controlling): “Bill, give me the keys or I get someone else to take me home.”


Bill (even more defensive): “Yeah, right! I notice you flirting with Jim all night long. I suppose you’d like a ride home with him.”


Wanda (in exasperation): Bill, just give me the keys.”
So here we have it. Wanda starts off in control mode. Bill defends by attacking Wanda with a fake-feeling judgment. The attack pushes Wanda’s buttons, and she escalates further with a threat. Let’s run it again using intimacy-centric relating with tact and diplomacy.


Wanda (establishing rapport): “Bill, thank you for taking me out dancing.”


Bill: “I like how you move with me, and I also like that all the men stare at you and wonder what I got that attracts you.”


Wanda (tactfully introducing safety): “Bill, I can tell you what you got. You make me feel safe letting go on the dance floor.”


Bill (enjoying feeling protective): “I like to keep my honey safe.”


Wanda (diplomatically): “You could make me feel even safer if you let me drive us home tonight.”


Bill: “Sure, baby! Good call! I do still feel a little buzz.”


Much better! Wanda can always choose between starting off in (1) control mode or (2) feelings mode with tact and diplomacy.


* Tact: showing consideration and avoiding offense
* Diplomacy: handling situations with skill and sensitivity 
* Tone: accent or inflection expressing a feeling
* Disingenuousness: slightly dishonest or not speaking the complete truth


Wanda uses tact and diplomacy sincerely and speaks proactively from her heart, not just medicinally from her judgment knots. Faking feelings and speaking disingenuously does not work in intimate relationships. Bill can read Wanda’s tone and can easily feel past her artful words.


Wanda sincerely cares about Bill and her relationship with him. She expresses her caring by employing tact and diplomacy.

Good Things




  



















https://www.familygoodthings.com/the-importance-of-family-dinner/ 




We generally run this process while we break bread.  


Each person takes about one minute to express good things they recall happening during the day. The other members listen actively without interrupting. At the end of his minute, the person gives thanks to whatever higher power he wishes to acknowledge. Everyone thanks the person for sharing, and then the process passes to the next person.


This process works to inspire each other with optimism. It also helps everyone stay positive and on the lookout during the day for something good to share at dinner.  


This process has similarities with saying grace, except everyone has a turn, everyone else listens actively, and everyone thanks each other.




Listening Without Judgment


  





















http://campuskids.net/nj2011/journals_pages/Improv.html 


People sit or stand in two columns (A and B) facing each other so each person has a partner across.


1. Tell Me What You Think


The basic transaction:


         (a) Partner A (the receiver) says, “Tell me what you think, now.”
(b) B (the sender) responds, such as “I don’t know what to think.”
(c) A (the receiver) listens without reacting and says, “Thank you.”


The partners then exchange roles so B (the new receiver) initiates the basic transaction. This completes one cycle.


The partners keep going back and forth completing more cycles.


While the partners cycle, the PM walks around the columns, and whenever he sees a receiver showing any form of judgment (either positive or negative), he says without judgment, “I see a receiver smiling and agreeing.” “I see a receiver showing disgust.” 


The goal: to learn how to listen to someone actively through several cycles without reacting. Active listeners listen; they do not send agreement or disagreement or interrupt the sender in any way. Most people find this exercise unnatural at first and eventually quite enlightening.   


At the end of this phase, the PM invites members to share their experiences with the Tribe.


2. Tell Me What You Feel


This variation follows the Tell-Me-What-You-Think Process format with the substitution of Feel for Think. This raises the stakes a bit as sharing feelings draws people out of their control-centric minds and into their intimacy-centric feelings.  


People who can listen to thoughts and ideas without judgment may find it difficult at first to listen to feelings without judgment.


3. Show Me What You Feel


This variation raises the stakes even higher. The receiver prompts with “Show Me How You Feel.” The sender replies by expressing body forms and without using language. He may not use words as part of a form; he may growl or howl or cry. 


People who make it through this variation emerge with the ability to listen actively.


At the end of these phases, the partners acknowledge each other. The participants then shift one space to the right so they face a different partner.  The people on the ends move over to the other side so everyone matches up.


In case of an odd number of members, the PM also takes part in the process as well as managing it.


The PM runs the entire set of three phases again. At the end, the partners acknowledge each other and shift again.


The PM keeps running the process over and over with different partners for about a half hour or 45 minutes or until everyone in the Tribe has a chance to share the experiences with all the other members. 


This process has several benefits:


1. Members learn how to listen actively.


2. Members come to trust each other to listen actively, and the level of trust and intimacy in the Tribe grows.


3.  People feel a direct caring connection with several other people in the room and, voila, the Tribe turns into a community. In a two-person Tribe, you can have one partner; you can complete this process in about three minutes.  In a three-person Tribe, we have three phases x six transactions per phase at fifteen seconds per transaction + sharing and moving ~ ten minutes. In a four-person Tribe, you can have three partners, so you can complete the process in about fifteen minutes. In an eight-person Tribe, the process takes a little over a half hour. You can process twelve people in about an hour. Above eight members, intimacy breaks down and the processes lose potency.


In a Workshop setting in which we may have more than twelve people, I break the room into about eight-person groups that can run simultaneously. Some of the members with experience may volunteer as PMs.


Get Off My Back


  

























https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bull_riding 


The PM divides the room into two-person teams, A and B.


The basic transaction:


        (a) A gets down on the floor on all fours like a dog or a bull. B places a towel on A’s back to prevent touching him directly.  
(b) B then stands next to A, presses down on the towel, and says, “I own you.” He says nothing more and nothing less.
(c) A replies, “Get off my back.” He says nothing more and nothing less.


The team keeps repeating steps (b) and (c) over and over, going deeper and deeper into the emotional reactions they have surrounding authority and submission and expressing those feelings in the tone of their responses.  


Presently, the PM announces, “Wind it up and change roles” and then, “Continue.”


After running the process for both partners (in a room with many pairs), the PM says, “OK, wrap it up, acknowledge your partner, and discharge your partner from his role.”


The PM then invites people to share their feelings about the process.


The PM may then have the Tribe form different couples and rerun the process until everyone has an opportunity to connect with their feelings about authority and submission.


This process accomplishes several things. It strengthens the emotional bonds between the people in pairs. By extension, this also strengthens the community. It helps people notice if they have any judgments about authority and submission. Noticing and untying judgment knots in these areas helps people deal with their bosses and employees more graciously and effectively.

Bumper Stickers
  



























www.Stickers.com 


The PM cuts standard 8.5" x 11" pieces of paper in half lengthwise into the shape of a bumper sticker.


The Tribe members make bumper stickers for one goal they wish to realize, such as I Weigh 185 Pounds or I Work Out Three Times Per Week or I Attend Church Every Sunday.


This requires getting clear on what they really want and also using only a few words.


The Tribe members then get up, hold their bumper stickers across their chests, and drive themselves around the room into the flow of traffic. When they encounter a bumper sticker, they say without judgment (1) “I get it” or (2) “I don’t get it.”


The Tribe members then return to their seats and consider the feedback. If people do not get it, they might not yet have their goal clear.


The Tribe members may then make a new bumper sticker. Meanwhile, the PM invites members to share their experiences of the process.  When everyone has their revision complete, the cars take off again for another drive.


The PM may run the process several times until everyone gets clarity on one specific goal and has the bumper sticker for it.


Everyone then takes a photo of their bumper sticker and posts it to their Tribe-Go account. After each Tribe meeting members post their progress toward their goal until they realize their goal (or abandon it) and post a new bumper sticker for a new goal.   


Members can see all the bumper stickers online, although the authors’ names remain anonymous, and they can click on them to see the history of progress toward the goals and offer feedback (anonymously) about the goals.


The Bumper Sticker Process gets to the heart of setting goals and realizing them. When you state a goal, you also create a problem—namely, the difference between what you say you want and what you have. If you want a problem-free life, you simply accept things as you now have them and spend your life in static bliss.


If you wish to accomplish anything, you have to generate a problem to solve and then start moving toward the goal. As you move along this path, various feelings come up. Eventually you encounter a feeling that you have in a knot.


Say you want to lose fifteen pounds. You start a diet and begin exercising.  Then you get a strong urge to watch TV and munch on a bowl of tortilla strips and spinach dip. If you have no judgment about that urge and no tasty-snack knot, you merely notice it and release it. If you have a judgment about that urge, you cannot release it; you have to make it go away. You have to medicate the feeling. You can either indulge the urge to break the diet or distract yourself with another drama (get drunk, start a fight, lose something).


In Tribe you have one additional and very important option. You can bring the feeling of wanting chips and dip to Tribe and untie the judgment knot.  Once the judgment disappears about hunger, you can notice it and respond to it by confirming your diet works.


Committing to a goal brings you face-to-face with your knots. At that point, you can quit or you can take the opportunity to work through your knots. To accomplish anything, commit to doing it, start doing it, and use the feelings that come up about it as opportunities to untie your knots. 


If you wait around to “feel like it,” you wind up waiting around forever.

Irate Customer


The Irate-Customer Process demonstrates the power of establishing rapport and active listening. We run this process in pairs, A and B. At first, A plays the irate customer, and B plays the customer service representative. Then they switch roles.
  























image credit: www.wimammoth.com/Shutterstock    


This scenario forms the basis for the process: Cynthia buys a new toaster, takes it home, plugs it in, and burns the toast. She tries again and gets the same result again and again. So she takes the toaster back to the store and, in an angry tone, tells Jim, the customer service guy, “This toaster doesn’t work right. It keeps burning the toast. A real piece of junk. I want my money back.”


Jim says, “OK, well, let’s see here. It says right here on the box you have to read the instructions before using the product. You know we get a lot of people who come in here thinking they know how to operate appliances without reading the instructions. You have no right to feel angry if you don’t follow the instructions. You probably have it set too high.”


This makes Cynthia even angrier. Her face turns red. She demands to talk to Jim’s boss.


Jim shrugs and pages Tom, who comes out of his office and says, “Thank you for visiting Customer Service. You seem angry. I wonder if you can tell me a little more about it.”  


Cynthia replies, “You bet I feel angry. First, the toaster doesn’t work, and then I come in here and Jim tells me I have no right to feel angry and that I don’t know how to read instructions.”


Tom: “Thank you for telling me how you feel. You have every right in the world to feel angry. We value you and want to keep your business. Of course you can have a refund.”


Cynthia’s anger dissipates and she feels calmer and happy to talk with Tom.


Cynthia:  “Thank you. By the way, you might consider sending Jim to charm school.”




You  Can  Intensify a Feeling 
by invalidating it.


You Can Dissipate a Feeling 
by validating it.
	



Jim judges and invalidates Cynthia’s anger and this blocks her expression so it builds up and intensifies; Tom validates her anger and helps her express it, and it dissipates.


Our feelings help us know what to do. Hunger tells us to eat. Thirst tells us to drink. Fear informs risk control. Anger signals compromise of our boundaries. Sadness helps us deal with loss. Jealousy helps us protect our property. Weariness tells us to rest. All in all, a great system. Well, almost.


In practice, we don’t always listen to our feelings. Indeed, we block them, invalidate them, suppress them, and judge them. This prevents us from following their guidance and pretty much gums up the works.


This also applies to personal relationships, business relationships, and even investing. In this way, our judgments determine our core personalities, our relationships with others, and the contours of our lives. And just as we see Jim and Tom changing Cynthia’s feelings and personality by employing invalidation and validation, we, too, can learn to evolve toward serving others by learning to manage our own judgments.


In the Irate-Customer Process, A plays the part of Cynthia, while B plays the part of Jim. Cynthia keeps complaining about the toaster, and Jim keeps finding ways to invalidate her. Cynthia grows more and more irate, while Jim grows more and more critical. 
  
Presently, Cynthia demands to talk to Jim’s boss. At that point, B switches from playing Jim to playing Tom. At first, Cynthia continues to complain. Tom listens actively and validates Cynthia’s feelings. Cynthia grows calmer while Tom continues to listen actively. At the end of the cycle, A and B take turns sharing their experiences of the process.


Then A and B switch roles and again act out the play. At the end of this cycle, A and B again take turns sharing their experiences of the process.


To conclude the process, A and B take turns discharging each other from their roles and welcoming each other back as friends and Tribe members. When all the couples complete the process, the PM invites a checkout in which each Tribe Member shares any insights from the process. The PM may also have the members switch partners and rerun the process.

Zero Point
This process intends to demonstrate how untying a judgment knot releases the feeling and the pain of the judgment and returns Hotseat to the zero point.
Hotseat: “I hate feeling sad about my breakup.”


Process Manager (testing for willingness): “I wonder if you have willingness to work on this issue.”


Hotseat: “I don’t want to feel my sadness. I want it to go away. And, yes, I have willingness to work on it.”


Process Manager (noticing forms): “OK. Squint your eyes more and notice the feelings in your body.”


Tribe members form the Healing Field of Acknowledgment and cheer him on.


Hotseat (grimacing and squirming): “OK. I can really feel it now, and I don’t like it.”


Process Manager (starting to untie some judgment): “OK, enjoy it and say ‘I like squinting my eyes. My sadness helps me to see loss early and to avoid it.’”
 
Hotseat (grudgingly): “OK, I like to squint. It protects me.”


Process Manager (validating the stack of judges): “Say, ‘I like judging my squinting and dwelling on it.’ And smile when you say that.”


Hotseat: “OK, I like judging my squinting and …” Upon smiling he bursts into laughter and receives cheers and congratulations from the other Tribe members.


Process Manager: “So how do you feel about your breakup?”


Hotseat: “I feel a little sad, and I feel OK about that. I hide my sadness for the last two years about this relationship. I guess my resistance to feeling sad keeps me from taking action for two years.”


Tribe members congratulate him for his breakthrough.
	

Time Line
The PM marks a long line on the floor with tape or twine. He then identifies one end of the line as the past and the other end as the future. He then asks the members if they would like to examine the past or future and cuts sections of the line and hands it to them. The members then talk about their experiences of getting to know their piece of past or future. At the end of the exercise, the PM reminds the participants that all the pieces exist in the moment of now.

Willingness Testing
In Tribe we continually test for willingness. We cannot force willingness on people. They either have it or they don’t. Some people willingly and enthusiastically participate in Tribe; others hold back and hesitate. Strict adherence to Willingness Testing protects the Tribe from energy dissipation and keeps it lively, attractive to new members, and transformative for existing members.  


Hotseat: “I don’t want to do this.”


Process Manager: “Great. Where do you have the feeling of not wanting to do it?”


Hotseat: “I have my hesitation in my shoulder.”


Process Manager: “I wonder if you feel willing to feel what you have in your shoulder.”


Case 1:


Hotseat: “Yes, I feel willing to go for it.

Case 2:

Drumming
We generally start Tribe meetings with drumming. The Tribe members sit in a drum circle facing each other. They all hold hand drums and beaters. The Chief generally has enough to go around. Some members bring their own drums. The Chief notices when all the members have their focus in the room. He then begins drumming a steady beat, about as fast as a heartbeat.


Other members key off the Chief’s beat or may improvise and add their own rhythms. At first, the drumming seems tentative and slightly out of sync. As members come to feel the communal heart beat and join in, the rhythm smooths out. Drumming celebrates a deep common organic connection between the members as the heart beats of the Tribe members synchronize. You can feel the Tribe slide into a trance as the members find themselves connecting emotionally and spiritually, as their minds become calm and quiet, and their spirits unite.


I recommend against using any kind of chemical “sacraments” prior to Tribe meetings; we can go deeper, develop stronger connections, and do better work without chemicals or drugs.

The Rocks Process
The Rocks Process consolidates most of the other processes into a comprehensive solution for members who wish to accelerate their progress in dealing with their issues and commit toward serving others.


The Rocks Process takes about an hour or two to run. In one Tribe Meeting we might run two or three of these processes. Over the course of a ten-week series, each Member of an eight-Member Tribe may have two or three opportunities to take the hot seat for a Rocks Process.


During the Rocks Process, the entire Tribe concentrates on Hotseat and his issues. As he tells his story, his associating feelings and emotions start to emerge. The other members identify with Hotseat, so they ride along and develop their own parallel insights into their own situations. The other Tribe members may also take part in role-playing, taking on the roles of parents, siblings, associates, and other important characters in Hotseat’s process. As such, they may deeply experience the drama from other points of view.  


The Chief may elect to conduct the Rocks Process himself or to appoint a PM to do it. In this way, the Chief can help the other members develop experience with leading processes and, ultimately, a Tribe.


Everyone must take the hot seat once for the Rocks Process before anyone can take the hot seat again. Then, everyone must take it twice before anyone can take it again.


The process includes fore-giving a medicinal rock and replacing it with a proactive rock. Fore-giving means to give a rock back to its donor (the person first teaching Hotseat how to use the rock). The process also includes identifying and untying judgment knots about the feeling.


Select Hotseat 
The Chief or PM generally asks who wants to work and then interviews the candidate who seems the most willing. 
Test For Willingness 
Process management includes frequent willingness testing. Whenever a process stalls, the PM tests Hotseat for willingness to proceed. If Hotseat proclaims willingness, the process proceeds; if not, the PM closes the process and moves on to the next Member who demonstrates willingness to work.  This avoids formation of co-dependent “helping” relationships between Hotseat and the Tribe.
Clarify the Issue 
The PM starts by asking Hotseat to clarify his issue. He requests that Hotseat define his problem or issue as the difference between (1) what he wants and (2) what he actually has.

Develop the Form 
Hotseat begins by describing his problem. As he proceeds, the PM invites him to experience and express the physical forms that associate with the issue. The other Tribe members notice the appearance of these forms or physical expressions such as jiggling a leg, rubbing the hands together, tightening the jaw, or squinting the eyes. They provide a Healing Field of Acknowledgment to encourage and amplify these forms.
“Good work!” “Do more of that.” “Jiggle your leg more.” “Yes! Rub your hands together harder.” “Way to go!” “Squint your eyes even tighter.” As the members provide the Healing Field of Acknowledgment, Hotseat manifests and amplifies a combination of motions, postures, expressions, and sounds.  As the encouragement proceeds, Hotseat also notices an internal gathering storm of feelings.  


The other members do not attempt to discuss or mention their guesses and opinions of Hotseat’s feelings or try to name them, as this can pull Hotseat out of his feelings trance and back into his conscious mind. Instead, they encourage Hotseat to amplify what they can actually see: his forms. This gives them the effective handle they need to support Hotseat in amplifying his emotions, forms, and feelings.


The PM must employ considerable skill and sensitivity not to overdo it. If he keeps encouraging Hotseat to intensify his forms, Hotseat may have a cathartic emotional release and sink into peaceful relaxation at the zero point.


Stop Before Zero
If Hotseat continues to experience his forms, he reaches a point of maximum intensity at which he abreacts (releases) his emotions and then feels a sense of relief. This returns him to the zero point.  


We use the Zero Point Process to illuminate the workings of the subconscious mind, the effects of pacing and establishing rapport with another person, and to provide short-term cathartic relief.  


If the PM takes Hotseat to the zero point, Hotseat may miss the window of opportunity to reprogram the Critical Incident.  Some Tribe members develop a taste for the Zero Point Process and use it as medication. They show up meeting after meeting with their “signature form” and request more medication.


A skillful PM can arrest the process just before the emotional peak. You might liken this skill to that of a trader pulling out of a market while the vast majority of traders frantically pile on to the buying frenzy or of a lover practicing birth control by precoital extraction.


Stopping just before abreaction connects Hotseat with the genesis of his issue, the critical event—an event, generally from childhood, in which people learn how to resolve stressful situations by copying someone else’s medicinal response.


* Freeze It: At that peak point, the PM stops encouraging Hotseat to increase his intensity and says, “Great! Now freeze it, amplify it, and feel it.” This informs the Tribe to stop cheering him on and to fall silent. It also allows Hotseat to experience his now-very-intense feelings. The PM then says, “Notice an event from your early life in which you feel just this way. Take whatever comes to mind.” This identifies a critical event.


A skillful PM with a skillful Tribe can guide the identification of a critical event in minutes; conventional talk therapy with unwilling clients might never make it through the client’s defenses and might never uncover the critical event. The Rocks Process bypasses the mind and its defenses and follows the forms and feelings directly to the critical event.


Once the PM identifies the critical event, he then asks Hotseat to describe the situation and all the key elements and people in that critical event. 


The Critical Event
At the point of maximum intensity, Hotseat has full connection with the feelings relating to his issue and easily recalls a similar and formative event from his youth. We call this the critical event since it provides access to the crucible in which Hotseat originally forms his responses to the stressful incidents and stores the information as knots and rocks.  


Hotseat, still deep in his emotional trance, then recounts whatever happens to pop up from his childhood in which he has these same forms and feelings.  


For example, John’s form includes rubbing his legs, squinting his eyes, and lowering his head. He easily recalls a critical incident in which his older brother talks back to his father. The father slaps his brother in the face and then whips him around the legs and back with the buckle end of his belt. His brother screams in anguish.   


Ever since the critical event, John cringes and has trouble dealing with authority figures. When his boss or wife yells at him, he feels his legs starting to itch, and he finds a way to run away from the situation like a child rather than confront it in an adult manner.


In this manner, the Tribe assists Hotseat in locating the parts of the body in which he stores the memories of critical events and the survival mechanisms he chooses for dealing with the critical events.  


Once the Tribe helps Hotseat open this corporal connection, the PM proceeds to guiding (1) the replacement of a medicinal rock with a proactive rock and (2) the untying of an active judgment knot. Thereafter, Hotseat, instinctively, subconsciously, and effortlessly responds to authority stress in a different way. Others pick up on the spirit of his new personality and find it difficult and inappropriate to yell at him.


Role-Play: Reproduce the Critical Event
The Tribe members listen as Hotseat describes an incident from his youth.  For example, Hotseat watches as his mother complains to her husband about his drinking and playing video games all evening and not participating with the family community.  


He sees his sister perk up and run away to her room, while his brother moves over to stand by his mother. As she continues complaining, her husband grows more and more angry, eventually slapping her and knocking her over onto the floor where she lies motionless except for sobbing. His brother goes over to her and lies down next to her. Hotseat recalls sinking into his chair, barely breathing, and wondering what to do. His father leaves the room.


The PM now invites the Tribe members to assume the various roles in the family constellation: father, mother, brother, sister.  


The role-players, including one Member playing the role of Hotseat, now assemble to re-create the original situation. The mother starts complaining; the father grows defensive and angry; the sister leaves; the brother moves to his mother; the father strikes the mother, and she falls to the floor, motionless; the brother lies down next to her. Hotseat sinks down into his chair, motionless, wondering what to do. The role-players aim to exactly reproduce the critical event. 


At the end of the role-play, the PM asks Hotseat if the role-play feels real. He generally says that not only do the role-players have the script right, they also have an uncanny ability to capture the way his family members say things to each other, even down to their body postures and expressions on their faces. 


In case Hotseat reports that something doesn’t quite fit in the role-play, such as the sister staying in the room a while longer or seeing the mother cover her face with her hands, the PM may instruct the Tribe to “run it again” until Hotseat confirms a very close match with the actual critical incident.


Rocks and Rock Donors
The Tribe uses physical rocks to model response patterns. A [fear → shut-down] rock makes you shut down when you feel fear. A [teasing → anger] rock guides you to get angry at someone who teases you. A [betrayal → get even] rock guides you to get even with someone who betrays you. A rock links a judgment knot (feeling + judgment) to a specific response. Rocks help us survive. We get our rocks from rock donors during critical events.


In our formative years when we encounter stressful situations, such as may frequently arise in control-centric and violent families, we look to our parents or other role models to see how they handle the stress. They teach us how to respond and, in Tribe parlance, act as our rock donor.


By this point in the process, the PM and the Tribe all have a pretty good idea who donates the rock. The mother shuts down; the son keeps her company; the sister avoids danger. In this case, Hotseat’s response seems to align best with the sister.  


Role-Play: Rock Replacement
The PM now calls for a re-play of the situation with rock replacement. At the high-stress point, when the father starts to slap the mother and the brother, the PM freezes the drama and has the sister (the rock donor) come forward and hand Hotseat a physical rock. She tells him, “Take this run-away rock. It can guide you and help you handle this situation. When you feel impending danger, you simply find an opportunity to disappear as soon as you can.”  

Hotseat takes the rock and thanks his sister. The role-play now continues. Hotseat, using the run-away rock, runs away to safety. He also hears his father slapping his mother and his brother in the other room.


Keep The Rock or Let it Go
The PM and the other Tribe members ask Hotseat to recount other instances in his life where he responds to danger by running away and deserting others.  He can think of dozens of instances.  


The PM then asks Hotseat if he wants to keep the run-away rock, or if he might like to return it to his sister and make space for a Heart Rock instead.


If Hotseat says he wants to keep the run-away rock, the PM closes the process. If Hotseat shows willingness to continue, the PM proceeds to Fore-Give the Medicinal (Run-Away) Rock.


Fore-Give the Medicinal Rock
Hotseat chooses to fore-give the rock; that means to give it back to the rock donor, in this case, the sister. Hotseat and the rock donor face each other.  Hotseat tells the rock donor sister, “Thank you for giving me this rock to protect me. I want to return it to you now and free myself of the instinctive desire to run away from danger. Running away doesn’t solve anything and just makes things worse. I want to try a different strategy.”


The rock donor sister shows resistance in receiving it back. She says, “I give you this rock to protect you. I use it myself for as long as I can remember. The [danger → run-away] rock protects you and keeps you safe.” By showing resistance to receiving it, she gives Hotseat an opportunity to muster up enough resolve to return his security blanket.


In this case, Hotseat goes for it and insists on fore-giving the rock; he stands over to his rock donor sister and places it in her hand. She says, “I hope you can survive without it. I don’t know how to survive without it.”


The Heart Rock
After Hotseat successfully returns the run-away rock, the Heart Rock donor (one of the other Tribe members) appears and proceeds to give Hotseat a Heart Rock. The Heart Rock donor explains to Hotseat, “As a consequence for fore-giving the run-away rock, I have a gift for you, a Heart Rock.” 


The proactive Heart Rock that replaces the medicinal [stress → run-away] rock contains a very powerful generic response to most interpersonal relationship situations. The Heart Rock pays off in many areas of life. It responds to stress by employing active listening and establishing emotional rapport and sharing feelings. We use the Heart Rock [stress→ establish rapport and share feelings] in many areas of Tribe work.


    •   The Heart Rock works like this: when you feel stress, you establish         emotional rapport with the stressor (the person who aims to hurt you), and you ask him how he feels. As you listen to his feelings, you notice him calming down, much like in the Zero-Point Process. When he         seems calm enough, you say, “I wonder if I may share my feelings with you.” If he agrees, you continue, “I feel afraid and also a little angry and protective of my mother and sister.” You do not blame him or go off into causality.             




The Heart Rock donor continues, “Also, the Heart Rock comes with a return policy; if you try it out and don’t like it, you may return it and get your original medicinal rock back, although no one ever does. The Heart Rock also comes with Tech Support; I can show you how to use it during role-playing, and even after the Tribe meeting, until you get the hang of it.”


Hotseat says, “Thank you” and explores the contours of the Heart Rock with his hands, beginning to absorb its pattern.










  

























In Tribe we use a special rock in the shape of a heart to represent the Heart Rock.  The Heart Rock converts interpersonal stress into these actions:


1. Engage active listening to establish rapport with the other person. Listen to the other person without judgment and let the other person know you appreciate and respect his feelings and points of view.


2. Gain permission to share your feelings with the other person, maintaining care to not blame him.


3. Keep the focus of the conversation on feelings.


4. Treat the other person with respect.


For people with relationship issues, the Heart Rock generally offers a substantial improvement over whatever other medicinal strategy they normally use. After the meeting, some Tribe members take the Heart Rock home and keep it on their person or on their desk as a souvenir and reminder.

I originally assemble the Trading Tribe to help traders deal with emotional issues. Since then, the Trading Tribe comes to serve as a way to build community, locate and deal with core issues, hold each other accountable for growth, and serve others.


In this section I mention some of the basic trading issues. 


Sam has his feelings about loss in a judgment knot. He does not like to take losses in the markets. He does not use stop-loss orders since they can lead to losing a position in a whipsaw just before the market turns up. Occasionally, a market has a small dip, then a larger one, and then an extra-large one that wipes out Sam’s account.


Emily willingly feels loss. She takes small losses early to protect against a market falling further. Her sensitivity to sadness also gives her a feel for markets that might generate losses. She takes her losses willingly and keeps her powder dry until the big one comes along.


Trey has a position in a stock, and he also has a judgment about fear; he does not like to feel fear. He has a fear knot. During a rapid rise in a stock, Trey naturally feels fear. The higher the market goes, the more he feels fearful, and the less he can stand it. At some point, he can stand it no longer, and he sells his position to medicate his feelings. This relieves his fear and also relieves him of his position in a strong bull market.  


Ryan does not have a position. He watches a stock rise and rise. He feels fear of missing out (FOMO), envy about other traders making money on the trade, and irritation at not having a position. He may have other feelings as well. In Tribe we don’t focus on naming the exact feelings since everyone feels and names feelings differently.  

If he has a judgment about any of the feelings about missing the move, he may arrive at the point where he can no longer stand the pain of the feeling of missing out. To medicate the pain, he throws caution to the wind and jumps into the market without much consideration of risk.


Mario, during a normal correction, notices he keeps losing money day after day after day after day. He has a judgment about the feeling of losing money during draw downs, and now and again he medicates his knot by selling out near the lows of a minor dip.


Most traders know the basic rules of trading: ride winners, cut losers, manage risk. Some traders use computer systems to follow these rules. Even system traders may have difficulty sticking to their own systems during volatile markets since they have their feelings about volatility in knots. They wind up medicating their knots by jumping off their systems. The trading rule “stick to the system” generally proves of little use against the strong urge to medicate a feeling.


Ultimately, In my experience, successful trading, like success in all areas, follows from clear intention to succeed. Clear intention follows from dissolving emotional knots, finding your calling, and dedicating to serving others.

The Ultimate Process: 
Applying TTP© in My Actual Life

I conduct all the processes above in Tribe meetings in which everyone knows the rules, has willingness to follow them, and enjoys the support of other Tribe members to stay on track.
I also enjoy some relationships with people who live by Tribe principles; we support each other in serving others and in helping to keep each other on track. 

The Ultimate Process, applying TTP© in my actual life outside of supportive Tribe meetings, where other people do not know about the rules, presents both challenges and opportunities.

I don’t have a definite algorithm for working the Ultimate Process. I can tell you that as I continue to work on it, using the stumble-forward-and-learn method, I seem to get a little better at it. 

When I encounter problems applying TTP© in the real world, I can pretty much trace it to my own failure to follow the principles, especially in the matter of allowing my own judgments to take over.  


Pushy Pushy Pushy

For example, in my enthusiasm to share TTP© with others and to base my relationships on the principles, I may cross over the line and start to push people into using the principles.  
* Ed: “I want you to tell me your feelings.”  
* Friend: “I feel like lunch.”
* Ed: “Lunch is not a feeling. Tell me how you feel about lunch.”
* Friend: “I feel like you want to make me wrong about the way I express myself.”
* Ed: “Tell me more about you feeling wrong.”
* Friend: “I feel like I’ve had just about enough of this. Good-bye.”


Later, my friend tells me she doesn’t like talking to me when I go off into Tribe mode, since she usually winds up feeling like I judge her and don’t accept her, and she has to say things “the right way” to please me. She feels like she has to “walk on eggshells” around me.

When I get a result like this, I have to remember the Ultimate Process.








The Ultimate Process


Whenever a relationship 
goes off track,
it always indicates
my own judgments.
	



In this case, when I finally get around to applying the Ultimate Process, I notice myself wanting to control more than to connect. I notice failing to test my friend for willingness to “go on the hot seat.” I notice myself wanting to “sell my ideas” rather than listen. I notice using manipulation and force. I notice a judgment about how my friend “should” communicate with me.

When I finally get off my high horse and return to humility, I notice my own judgments and failure to follow the principles. I see a better, more gentle, and effective way to honor the relationship, to deepen emotional connection, and to help my partner feel safe in sharing her feelings with me:
* Ed: “I want you to tell me your feelings.”  
* Friend: “I feel like lunch.”
* Ed: “Hmmm, me too. I wonder if you have a place in mind.”


And so, I continue to stumble forward, one baby step after another, practicing alternatives to the causal-judgment model and perhaps getting a little better at applying the lessons I learn from my Tribe.

Now and again, when I mind my cookies and actually follow the principles, I get pretty amazing results and enjoy deeply intimate and ecstatic connections.  Miracles keep showing up, and I feel enchantment right here in my condo in the Garden of Eden.
